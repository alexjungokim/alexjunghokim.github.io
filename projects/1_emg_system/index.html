sEMG Decoder System Design

End-to-end sEMG signal acquisition, processing, and neural decoding for wrist pose, discrete gestures, and handwriting -- based on the CTRL-Labs research platform.

Electromyography Signal Acquisition and Processing

Electrical activities generated by skeletal muscles represent the core EMG signal. These signals are generated from motor neurons which are part of the central nervous system.

  • Amplitude is the positive peak to negative peak voltage
  • Phase is the time duration of the initial negative cycle
  • Rise time is the time interval between negative and positive peaks
  • There are three turns in the EMG signal; the duration is defined as the total time between two negative cycles
  • Satellite is a small signal followed by the main EMG signal
  • Advantage of EMG over ECG and EEG is that ECG and EEG signals are below 100 Hz whereas EMG signals cover the range from 5 Hz to 2kHz

A Smart Approach to EMG Envelope Extraction and Powerful Denoising for HMI

EMG signals largely affected by powerline interference and motion artifacts. Feed-forward comb (FFC) filters can remove both powerline interferences and motion artifacts from EMG.

Feedforward Comb Filter Equation:

\[y(k) = x(k) + \alpha x(k-N)\]

Where x is input signal, N is delay expressed in number of samples, and \(\alpha\) is parameter that regulates filter behavior.

CTRL-LABS: Generic Noninvasive Neuromotor Interface for HCI

  • sEMG device (wearable) is a dry-electrode, multi-channel recording platform with 2kHz sample rate
  • Deployed neural networks trained on data from thousands of participants
  • Decoding tasks: 1D continuous navigation (wrist posture), gesture detection (finger pinches + thumb swipes), handwriting transcription
  • Achieved >90% classification accuracy for handwriting and gesture detection, >75% for wrist pose classification

Data Collection

  • Continuous navigation: participants prompted to flex, extend, or deviate their wrist
  • Discrete gestures: nine distinct gestures with randomized order and inter-gesture interval
  • Handwriting: participants write prompted text while holding fingers together

Single-participant Models do not Generalize Across Individuals

  • Models trained/tested on same participant improved with more data (median CLER 50.6% with 2 sessions to 26.5% with 4 sessions)
  • Models trained on one participant and tested on another showed substantially worse performance
  • t-SNE analysis showed no clear clusters of participants, indicating significant domain shift across people

Generic Models

  • Trained on data from thousands of participants to generalize
  • Performance follows inverse power law as a function of parameters and data quantity, consistent with LLM scaling properties
  • Reliable improvements with increasing number of training participants

Personalizing Handwriting Models

  • Fine-tuning generic model’s parameters using additional supervised data from held-out participants
  • Fine-tuning improved average CER across all data amounts and pre-training sizes
  • Personalization significantly improved performance of poorly performing participants
